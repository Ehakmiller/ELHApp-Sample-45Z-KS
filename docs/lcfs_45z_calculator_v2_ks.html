Note – 45Z

The Section 45Z Clean Fuel Production Credit is a federal tax incentive that rewards producers of transportation fuels based on lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions. Fuels with lower carbon intensity receive larger per-gallon credits, creating a direct financial incentive to reduce emissions through process improvements, feedstock changes, carbon capture, and low-carbon agricultural practices. The credit applies to qualifying fuels produced in the United States for use as transportation fuel and is currently scheduled to run from 2025 through 2027, replacing prior biofuel tax credit programs.

The credit is earned on each gallon of eligible fuel produced and sold for transportation use. Producers can use the credit to offset federal tax liability or transfer it to other taxpayers under credit transfer provisions, converting it into cash value. Because the credit is calculated per gallon, total financial benefit depends on both the per-gallon rate and the facility’s annual production capacity.

Note – 45Q

The Section 45Q Carbon Sequestration Credit is a federal tax incentive designed to encourage the capture and permanent storage of carbon dioxide emissions. Facilities that capture CO₂ and either store it in secure geological formations or utilize it in approved industrial processes can receive a per-ton tax credit. Higher credit values are provided for CO₂ that is permanently sequestered underground, creating a strong financial incentive for investments in carbon capture and storage (CCS) infrastructure.

The credit applies to qualifying carbon capture projects located in the United States and is available for twelve years after a carbon capture facility is placed in service. For projects that permanently sequester captured carbon dioxide in geological formations, the credit value is currently set at up to $85 per metric ton of CO₂. Because the credit is earned on each ton of carbon captured and stored, the total financial benefit depends on both the amount of CO₂ captured annually and the duration of the credit eligibility period following project start-up. Utilization pathways, including Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR), qualify for a lower credit value of up to $60 per metric ton, as these uses may not ensure the same level of permanent carbon storage as dedicated geologic sequestration.

Model Mechanics
  • Starts with a facility carbon intensity in gCO2e/MJ (LCFS CI or proxy estimate).
  • Applies adjustments (freight, ILUC, CCS, agricultural practices, misc) in gCO2e/MJ. Checked boxes are included in the calculations.
  • Converts to the 45Z policy unit kgCO2e/MMBtu at the final step for rate mapping.
  • Uses Benchmark and Max Rate to compute a raw emissions factor, rounds that factor to the nearest 0.1, and maps the rounded factor to a $/gal credit rate.
  • Calculates annual value as rate × gallons using ethanol capacity.
  • Starting CI rule: use California LCFS CI when available; otherwise use Oregon CI if California is absent; otherwise use technology proxy.
  • If Oregon is used, the delivery hub is fixed to Portland for freight normalization.
  • The tool estimates FOB CI by subtracting freight CI per mile × distance × multiplier, less the 300-mile baseline.
  • 45Z proxy CI = starting CI − freight (if checked) − ILUC (if checked) − CCS reduction (if checked) − agricultural reduction (if checked) − misc reduction (if checked, by your chosen sign convention).
  • $ /gal policy mapping is parameterized and editable in V1.

Program summary
For ethanol, California’s LCFS assigns carbon intensity using full lifecycle analysis that includes ILUC, significantly increasing CI scores for crop-based pathways like corn. Oregon’s Clean Fuels Program follows a similar framework but generally excludes ILUC, resulting in lower CI scores for the same ethanol plant and pathway. Both programs include transportation emissions, but California typically models longer, destination-specific rail routes, while Oregon often reflects shorter delivery to Portland, further widening the CI advantage.

Additionally, California allows highly customized, plant-specific pathways (for example fiber, CCS, and RNG tweaks), while Oregon relies more on approved/default pathways, offering less flexibility to optimize CI.
